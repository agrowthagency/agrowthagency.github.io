The weakest agency contracts sound strongest. They promise full ownership, unlimited support, compliant campaigns, and account replacement without defining one platform ID, permission, or decision owner.

## Attach a platform schedule

For each network, list manager/Business Center/Portfolio IDs, advertiser accounts, owner/controller, partner relationships, public identities, data assets, payment setup, verification, qualifications, and support channel.

Update the schedule when assets change. A contract detached from the live hierarchy becomes fiction.

## Define ownership precisely

State technical control, contractual rights, data rights, payer responsibility, and transferability per asset. Identify which assets cannot be guaranteed to transfer. Avoid saying the client “owns the account” when the provider’s organization controls it and the platform offers no assured transfer route.

## Build a responsibility matrix

| Area | Client | Agency | Provider/platform |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Business/product truth | Accountable | Validate for campaign use | Decides platform eligibility |
| Creative/media | Approves facts | Produces and operates | Reviews/enforces |
| Access/security | Maintains client admins | Controls agency users | Provides permission system |
| Billing | Funds/approves | Reconciles/invoices | Charges approved payer |
| Incident | Supplies client evidence | Coordinates response | Retains decision authority |

The contract cannot transfer platform decision authority to the agency.

## Write measurable agency SLAs

Measure agency-controlled actions: notification after detection, initial classification, containment start, evidence request, client update frequency, financial reconciliation, access removal, report delivery, and offboarding completion.

Do not guarantee platform review timing, approval, reinstatement, limit, or support response. Distinguish “agency responds within four hours” from “platform resolves within four hours.”

## Address money explicitly

Define media spend, service fee, taxes, exchange rate, prefunding, credit, threshold, invoice timing, unused balance, refunds, charge disputes, unauthorized spend, and insolvency. Identify the legal payer and client evidence.

## Cover data and security

Specify permissions, authentication, subcontractors, data purposes, sharing, client access, breach/incident notice, logs, retention, deletion, audit rights, and return. Name critical assets and exports.

## Define suspension and support boundaries

Set who preserves evidence, contacts the platform, approves appeals, speaks to the client, and handles affected shared systems. State that outcomes remain with the platform and prohibit bypass, false documents, or undisclosed replacement accounts.

## Make exit executable

Include notice, overlap, administrators, billing transfer, assets, reports, creative, data, support cases, balances, user/partner removal, deletion, and unresolved dependency disclosure.

This is operational information, not legal advice. Qualified counsel should adapt terms to jurisdiction and business model.

The contract’s value is not its confidence. It is whether a new operator can execute it without guessing.

## Require evidence and audit rights

Operational promises need observable artifacts: access rosters, approval records, billing reconciliations, incident timelines, subcontractor lists, and deletion confirmations. Define reasonable inspection rights, notice, confidentiality, sampling, remediation, and cost allocation. A broad audit clause without accessible evidence is mostly ceremonial.

## Govern subcontractors and upstream providers

Require disclosure of material providers, minimum control obligations, confidential treatment, incident notification, and responsibility for their actions. Define whether the agency may change providers without consent and what happens when an upstream relationship ends. The client should understand concentration without receiving another party’s confidential contract.

## Establish a review calendar

Review the platform schedule and controls when ownership, payer, provider, regulated status, markets, data flows, or platform terms materially change—and at a fixed interval even when nothing appears to change. Assign an owner for amendments so the contract does not drift behind the operating model.

## Control material changes

Specify which changes require client approval: new business containers, payer changes, data sharing, subcontractors, white-label supply, ownership changes, or substantially different support routes. Emergency changes can use rapid notice and retrospective review, but should not become a way to bypass consent.

## Match remedies to controllable failure

Service credits or termination rights should attach to failures the agency controls, such as missed notices, unperformed reconciliations, unauthorized access, or incomplete offboarding. Platform enforcement and review time require cooperation and communication commitments, not guaranteed outcomes.

## Convert the agreement into a control register

Extract every operational obligation into a register with the responsible party, due event, required evidence, dependency, escalation, and remedy. Examples include access reviews, billing statements, policy approvals, incident notices, verification renewals, provider disclosures, and exit delivery. Review overdue items with account leadership and the client. This prevents a carefully negotiated agreement from becoming a document consulted only after failure, when neither party can show how its promises were meant to operate.
