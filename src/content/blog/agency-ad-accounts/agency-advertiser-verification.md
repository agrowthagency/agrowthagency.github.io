Agencies often become the person completing verification without becoming the business being verified. Confusing those roles is one of the fastest ways to turn legitimate complexity into apparent misrepresentation.

## Map the operating identities

Record:

1. Legal advertiser.
2. Brand owner.
3. Product or service provider.
4. License or certificate holder.
5. Domain and landing-page operator.
6. Agency and subcontractor.
7. Platform-account controller.
8. Payer and invoice recipient.
9. Customer-contracting and data-recipient entities.

For every difference, keep a contract, corporate record, authorization, license, or billing explanation.

## Verification scope differs by platform

Google distinguishes advertiser and business-operations verification, payment profiles, manager relationships, and advertiser disclosures. TikTok supports business verification in Ads Manager or Business Center and distinguishes company and representative information. Meta business and Page identity processes sit alongside business assets and advertiser relationships.

Do not describe a verified Business Center, manager, or agency as though all client advertisers inherit a transferable status.

## Build a canonical client record

| Field | Source of truth |
| --- | --- |
| Legal name/number | Current authoritative registry |
| Address/contact | Accepted current business record |
| Domain/brand | Ownership, license, or client authorization |
| Representative | Named role and authority |
| Agency relationship | Current contract and scope |
| Payer | Finance agreement and platform record |
| Qualification | Issuer, activity, market, products, expiry |

Normalize legitimate name, translation, and address differences without altering documents.

## The agency should coordinate, not impersonate

The client supplies legal identity, product, qualifications, and customer facts. The agency supplies campaign, account, access, and operational facts. A provider supplies its billing or platform relationship. One case owner reconciles them before submission.

Never use the agency’s documents as if they license or identify the client’s unrelated activity. Never source “verification documents” detached from the actual entity.

## Diagnose rejection with a comparison table

List submitted value, document value, discrepancy, explanation or correction, evidence owner, and status for every field. Preserve the rejection and platform route. Avoid cycling through unrelated documents until one passes.

## Govern transitions

Rebrands, acquisitions, agency changes, payer changes, domains, representatives, and licenses can invalidate a previously coherent record. Define change triggers and preserve old-to-new continuity evidence.

When an agency exits, the client should retain the verified record, documents, IDs, deadlines, support cases, and administrators needed to reproduce its identity.

Use [pre-verified ad accounts](/agency-ad-accounts/pre-verified-ad-accounts/) to assess commercial verification claims and [agency policy compliance governance](/agency-ad-accounts/agency-policy-compliance-governance/) for ongoing operations.

## Build an evidence-to-claim map

For every identity statement shown to a platform—legal name, address, domain control, payment responsibility, regulated status—record the authoritative evidence and its owner. This exposes contradictions before submission. A certificate can support legal existence, for example, but may not prove control of the advertised domain or explain why a different entity pays.

## Handle corporate change as a new proposition

A merger or reorganization is not a document-refresh exercise. Re-evaluate which entity advertises, contracts with the agency, owns the site, fulfills orders, and funds media. Decide whether the existing account relationship still reflects reality before uploading new records. Preserve the change narrative so reviewers can understand why old and new evidence differ.

## Protect verification documents

Collect only what the platform and operating model require. Restrict access, set retention periods, log external sharing, and use approved transfer channels. Copies of directors’ IDs and corporate records should not sit indefinitely in campaign folders accessible to the whole buying team.

## Treat shared verification as a dependency

If a provider’s or agency’s verified organization is part of the setup, document what the verification covers and what remains the client’s responsibility. The client still needs truthful destinations, business disclosures, and evidence for its own claims. Shared verification is not portable proof that every downstream advertiser has been approved.

The governance output should be a current identity record, evidence register, expiry calendar, discrepancy log, and named decision owner—not merely a screenshot showing “verified.”

## Review verification readiness

Use a readiness view containing identity discrepancies, evidence owners, document expiry, domain and payment alignment, regulated permissions, pending platform requests, corporate changes, and transition dependencies. Platform owners should connect it to the procedures for [Google advertiser verification](/google-ads/google-ads-advertiser-verification/), [Meta business verification](/meta-ads/meta-business-verification/), or [TikTok business verification](/tiktok-ads/tiktok-business-verification/) rather than assuming one status translates across systems. Test whether an authorized client representative—not only the agency—can reproduce the record. Verification status is lagging; readiness shows whether the underlying facts remain coherent when the platform asks again.
