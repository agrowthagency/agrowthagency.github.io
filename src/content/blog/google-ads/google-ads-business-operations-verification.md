Business Operations Verification often fails even when every submitted company is real.

The parent is registered. The agency exists. The supplier has a contract. The website is live. The payer is solvent. But the submission never explains why the parent pays, the subsidiary advertises, the agency owns the account, the supplier fulfills the order, and a founder owns the domain.

Google Ads Business Operations Verification is not a search for the strongest document. It is a test of whether those documents describe one comprehensible operating model.

## BOV is a task within Advertiser Verification

Google’s current [Advertiser Verification guidance](https://support.google.com/adspolicy/answer/9703665?hl=en) describes a broader program with tasks that vary by account, billing setup, advertiser type, and activity. Business Operations Verification is the task focused on how the business operates and how relevant organizations participate.

Keep the distinction clear:

- Identity or organization verification asks who the person or entity is.
- Business Operations Verification asks what the business does and who performs the material roles.
- Billing verification asks about payer or payment responsibility.
- Vertical certification asks whether an entity and offer are eligible in a regulated category or location.

These processes can interact, but passing one does not answer all the others.

## Build the operating model before opening the form

Create a one-page entity map covering:

| Role | Questions |
| --- | --- |
| Advertiser | Which entity is accountable for the advertising and claims? |
| Product provider | Who supplies or performs the product or service? |
| Seller | Who contracts with and charges the customer? |
| Domain owner | Who owns or is authorized to operate the destination? |
| Agency | Who manages campaigns and what authority do they have? |
| Payer | Who is legally responsible for Google Ads costs and why? |
| Brand owner | Who owns the public-facing name and marks? |
| License holder | Which entity holds required authorization in the target market? |

Add legal names, trading names, countries, websites, customer IDs, payments profile, and evidence for each relationship. Then compare the map to the live account and website.

If the internal team cannot agree on the map, the form is not ready.

## Relationship evidence must explain the material role

Google’s current [BOV issue guidance](https://support.google.com/adspolicy/answer/11938893?hl=en) identifies insufficient information about business relationships, providers of goods or services, agencies, domain owners, and other parties accessing the account.

Useful evidence can include:

- Client-agency authorization.
- Supplier, reseller, affiliate, franchise, or fulfillment agreement.
- Parent-subsidiary records or intercompany authorization.
- Domain ownership or license to operate the site.
- Brand authorization.
- Invoice or commercial correspondence supporting a relationship.
- License and regulator record.
- Public verification source or unique business identifier.

The document should support the claimed role. A generic invoice may show that two companies transact but not that one is authorized to advertise under the other’s brand. A registration certificate proves existence but not supplier or agency authority.

Redact proprietary information only to the extent that the material relationship remains understandable.

## Document quality is part of the answer

Even the correct evidence can fail operationally when it is incomplete, illegible, expired, or detached from the answer it supports. Build a submission index that names each file, issuing entity, relevant fields, relationship proved, date, and any redaction.

Check that legal names include the correct suffixes, addresses match the intended entity, all material pages and signatures are present, and licenses remain current. Avoid unexplained file collections when the task requests formal evidence. If translated evidence is necessary, retain the original and make the relationship between versions clear.

The reviewer should not have to infer why a document was uploaded. Evidence architecture is part of business-model clarity.

## The website is evidence, not decoration

Reviewers can compare form answers with the destination. The site should make the commercial model legible:

- Legal seller or service provider.
- Brand and trading-name relationship.
- Contact and support responsibility.
- Product source where material.
- Pricing, delivery, cancellation, and refund terms.
- Required licenses or qualifications.
- Third-party relationships the site implies.

Do not add an entity to the footer solely to match a document while checkout, terms, support, and payment describe someone else. Website changes should correct business truth, not stage a temporary verification version.

## Common failure patterns are structural

### The provider is unclear

An affiliate or reseller describes the offer but does not identify who actually supplies it. The form names one entity and customer terms name another.

### Relationships are undisclosed

The website mentions brands, agencies, or partners that the submission does not explain. Account access reveals another party with no stated role.

### Business type is wrong

The advertiser selects direct provider while operating as an agency, marketplace, lead generator, or affiliate. Every later answer inherits the wrong model.

### Entity fields do not reconcile

Legal name, disclosure, payments country, domain identity, and license holder describe different entities without an evidence chain.

### Documents answer the wrong question

The team uploads more incorporation records when Google needs evidence of authorization, supply, licensing, or domain relationship.

The remedy is not document volume. It is correspondence between question, answer, and proof.

## A composite scenario: the agency that became the advertiser

Consider a composite agency that creates the client’s Google Ads account, pays media through a central arrangement, controls the landing page, and submits its own company documents for verification. The client supplies the regulated service and holds the license.

The agency is a legitimate business, but the submission makes it appear to be the service provider. The website uses the client brand, while the account and payment records point to the agency. Verification fails because the operating roles are collapsed.

The stronger submission identifies the client as the regulated provider, documents the agency’s management and billing authority, shows the client’s license and brand ownership, and explains the domain arrangement. The account structure is adjusted where necessary so direct client control and disclosure remain credible.

The objective is not to remove the agency from view. It is to represent the agency accurately.

## Treat failed verification as a versioned incident

Preserve:

- Exact issue message.
- Questions and answers submitted.
- Documents and versions.
- Submitter and administrator role.
- Account, website, billing, and ownership state at submission.
- Changes made before the next attempt.
- Outcome and remaining inconsistency.

Do not let different employees submit incompatible explanations. Assign one owner and have legal, finance, operations, and the agency validate their respective facts.

Where an account is paused, follow the task and current support route shown in the account. Avoid creating a replacement account or supplying false information to bypass verification; that can create Circumventing systems risk.

## BOV readiness is business-model readiness

Before submission, confirm that the organization can answer three sentences without qualification:

1. This is the advertiser.
2. These parties perform these roles for these documented reasons.
3. The account, website, payments, licenses, and public disclosure reflect that model.

Complexity is not automatically suspicious. Unexplained complexity is fragile.
