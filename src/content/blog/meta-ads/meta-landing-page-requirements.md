“The landing page loads for me” is not a compliance test.

It establishes that one person reached one version at one moment. A Meta ad can send people through tracking parameters, redirect services, consent layers, localization, device rules, experiments, and personalized content before the final offer appears.

Meta’s [ad review guide](https://www.facebook.com/business/ads/review-policy-guidelines) states that review may include an ad’s destination. The managed object is therefore the complete post-click experience, not only the URL typed into Ads Manager.

## Reconstruct the complete click path

For every production ad, resolve:

`ad URL → tracking template → redirect hosts → consent/interstitial → final page → form, checkout, app store, or message`

Test from the targeted countries and representative mobile devices. Record response codes, redirect hosts, rendered content, security warnings, page ownership, and the final action requested from the user.

Check whether crawlers or users encounter:

- Broken, slow, or inaccessible pages.
- A different domain or materially different offer.
- Forced downloads, deceptive controls, or unexpected pop-ups.
- Missing product, price, eligibility condition, or recurring-payment disclosure.
- Claims that do not match the ad.
- Forms requesting data unrelated to the stated service.
- Malware, compromised scripts, or certificate failures.
- Geographic or device variation that changes important facts.

## Truth must remain stable across the click

Create a claim map linking every material ad claim to destination evidence.

| Ad claim | Destination requirement |
| --- | --- |
| Price or discount | Same price, clear conditions, and real availability |
| Outcome or performance | Support, qualification, and no contradictory fine print |
| Brand or authority | Clear operator and genuine relationship |
| “Free” action | Transparent later charges or commitments |
| Limited availability | Accurate timing, inventory, and eligibility |
| Regulated service | Required license, geography, audience, and disclosures |

Fine print should clarify a truthful primary message, not reverse it. The Page, ad, domain, checkout descriptor, and legal business should form a relationship a reasonable user can understand.

## Web releases are advertising changes

An approved ad can point to a destination that changes later. Connect the ad inventory to web deployment records. High-risk changes include pricing, forms, redirect logic, tag-manager scripts, domain migrations, experiments, third-party widgets, and localized templates.

Assign owners across paid media, web, legal/compliance, analytics, security, and product. A release should identify which live ads and markets it affects. Maintain rollback capability and screenshots of the reviewed state.

## Diagnose incidents across conditions

When ads are rejected or accounts restricted, test the destination from multiple networks, locations, and devices. Expand shortened URLs and inspect every host. Review recent DNS, CDN, CMS, consent, analytics, and affiliate changes. Scan for compromise if the page or redirect behavior is unfamiliar.

Do not respond by swapping domains while leaving the same defective experience in place. That can turn a fixable destination problem into a broader question about review evasion or business integrity.

For the rejected ad decision, use [Meta ads rejected](/meta-ads/meta-ads-rejected/). For identity mismatches, continue with [Meta business verification](/meta-ads/meta-business-verification/). If unexpected redirects or content suggest compromise, use [Meta ad account security](/meta-ads/meta-ad-account-security/) and investigate the website separately.

## Test more than the home page

Ads often land on product pages, deep links, app stores, lead flows, checkout states, or URLs generated by a feed. Build a destination inventory from live ads rather than testing only the brand’s main domain.

Sample every template and high-risk market. Include expired products, out-of-stock states, invalid parameters, consent rejection, slow networks, logged-out users, and unsupported locations. Error and fallback states are part of the customer experience.

## Manage redirects and tracking

List every first-party and third-party host in the click path, its owner, purpose, data behavior, contract, and failure mode. Monitor for new redirect domains and unexpected changes. Shorteners or affiliate trackers should not obscure the final business or deliver materially different content.

When a tracking vendor changes, retest response codes, parameter encoding, mobile behavior, consent, final URL, and security. Marketing should not deploy a new redirect layer without web and security ownership.

## Review data collection after the click

Forms, chat, quizzes, checkout, and pixels should collect data consistent with the stated offer and the organization’s privacy disclosures. Identify which entity receives the data, why each field is necessary, how consent is handled, and where the information goes next.

Sensitive or regulated data flows require legal and privacy review. A visually compliant ad does not cure an inappropriate post-click collection practice.

## Detect content drift

Create automated or scheduled checks for final URLs, response status, domain changes, material page text, price, product availability, security warnings, and redirect chains. Pair alerts with a human owner who can compare the change to live ads.

Keep a release annotation showing which campaign IDs rely on the page or template. This allows a web owner to find affected ads quickly rather than asking every media buyer to search manually.

## Investigate cloaking-like variation carefully

Variation can be legitimate: localization, inventory, authentication, experimentation, or accessibility. Document its rule and ensure it does not hide material offer terms from reviewers or users. Compare results across IP location, device, user agent, cookies, referral parameters, and authentication state when behavior differs.

If a third party controls the variation, preserve logs and escalate contractually. Do not disable the rule only during review and restore it later.

## Define destination acceptance criteria

A release is ready for ads only when it is accessible in targeted conditions, accurately represents the offer and business, includes necessary terms, uses approved claims, handles errors honestly, protects users, and has a rollback owner. Store the evidence with the campaign preflight packet.

For shop and catalog traffic, reconcile these destination controls with the product and fulfillment evidence behind a [restricted Meta commerce account](/meta-ads/meta-commerce-account-restricted/).
